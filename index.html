Score Snapshot

The IWA rubric totals 48 points, scored row by row.

Row 1 5 Stimulus integration
Row 2 5 Context & significance
Row 3 9 Perspectives
Row 4 12 Argument
Row 5 9 Evidence
Row 6 5 Citations
Row 7 3 Academic style
⚠️

A response scores 0 across all rows if it is off-topic, a repetition of the prompt, entirely crossed out, a drawing or other markings, a presentation or off-task format, or written in a language other than English. A response that does not connect to a theme across at least two stimulus materials is off-topic. Citing only prior-year stimulus materials is also off-topic. Blank submissions receive NR (No Response).

Bootcamp Roadmap

Follow this rubric-driven sequence to keep every paragraph working for points.

1

Connect to the Stimulus

Integrate at least one stimulus source so it enhances, forwards, or directly supports your argument. A passing mention, a bare definition, or a reference that could be deleted without effect earns 0 points in Row 1.

2

Establish Context & Significance

Situate your research question in a specific, focused context with clear who/what/where/when details and a compelling case for urgency or importance. Keep the scope narrow enough to address the complexity of the problem.

3

Evaluate Perspectives

Identify perspectives from distinct sources — not general stakeholder labels. To score 9 points, evaluate connections among perspectives, address objections, implications, and limitations with consistent source attribution.

4

Build the Argument

Drive your argument with your own voice and commentary — not evidence alone. Organize claims logically, explain how evidence supports each claim, and reach a conclusion fully aligned with your research question.

5

Select & Use Evidence

Use relevant, credible, and sufficient evidence. Include well-vetted sources beyond the stimulus (scholarly, peer-reviewed, government, or credentialed authors). Encyclopedias and dictionaries do not count.

6

Apply Conventions — Citations

Use any consistent citation style. Every in-text citation or footnote must link to a bibliography entry. Missing either your bibliography or your in-text citations earns a 0 for Row 6.

7

Apply Conventions — Academic Style

Write with sentence variety, precise word choice, and a consistently academic tone. Scorers evaluate only your own sentences — not quoted material. A few minor errors are acceptable at the top band.

Section-by-Section Guides

Detailed directions for each row so you know exactly what earns points — and what doesn't.

Row 1

Stimulus Integration

0 or 5 points

Goal: Demonstrate the relevance of at least one stimulus by integrating it meaningfully into your argument.

Do this ✅

  • Show an accurate understanding of the source and its context (date, region, topic).
  • Use the stimulus so it enhances, forwards, or directly supports your argument.
  • You only need one successful integration — but it must be purposeful, not perfunctory.
  • Connect the stimulus to your research question or a specific claim.

Avoid ❌

  • Mentioning a stimulus tangentially or only for a definition.
  • Using a stimulus that could be deleted with little or no effect on your argument.
  • Misrepresenting the source or stripping it of its context.
  • Citing only prior-year stimulus materials (this makes the entire response off-topic).
Sentence frame: One stimulus highlights ___, which matters because ___; this connects to my research question by ___.
Row 2

Context & Significance

0 or 5 points

Goal: Situate your research question in a specific, focused context and make a compelling case for its urgency or importance.

Do this ✅

  • Address an area narrow enough to handle complexity (e.g., "water pollution in India," not "air, water, and land pollution").
  • Provide specific who/what/where/when details for all elements of your question.
  • Make a specific, compelling case for the urgency or importance of the issue.
  • Keep the context relevant throughout the entire argument — not just the intro.

Avoid ❌

  • Overly broad topics that try to address too many aspects at once.
  • Vague assertions like "this is important" without explanation.
  • Providing context for only part of the research question.
  • Contextual details that are tangential to your argument.
Sentence frame: This issue affects ___ in ___ during ___, and it matters because ___.
Row 3

Perspectives

0 / 6 / 9 points

Goal: Evaluate multiple source-attributed perspectives by drawing connections, considering objections, implications, and limitations.

📌 A perspective is "a point of view conveyed through an argument" — it must come from a specific source's argument, not a general stakeholder label, topic, or lens.

Do this ✅

  • Attribute each perspective clearly to its specific source with in-text citations.
  • For 9 pts: evaluate connections, compare strengths and weaknesses, and address objections, implications, or limitations.
  • Use transitions to explicitly signal connections between perspectives.
  • For 6 pts: describe similarities or differences — even general comparison earns partial credit.

Avoid ❌

  • Expressing perspectives as personal opinions without source attribution (risks a 0).
  • Using multiple lenses that all convey the same point of view.
  • Listing perspectives in separate sections without connecting them.
  • Oversimplifying by treating many voices or stakeholders as one perspective.
Sentence frame: [Source A] argues ___, while [Source B] contends ___. The tension between these perspectives reveals ___, which suggests ___.
Row 4

Argument

0 / 8 / 12 points

Goal: Build a clear, logically organized, well-reasoned argument driven by your own commentary, leading to a plausible conclusion fully aligned with your research question.

Do this ✅

  • Drive the argument with your own voice and commentary — not evidence summaries.
  • Signpost your organization explicitly so the structure is easy to follow.
  • Provide commentary that engages with the content of evidence to draw conclusions.
  • Make the conclusion fully aligned with the research question and detailed enough to assess plausibility.

Avoid ❌

  • Summary or report-style writing that takes no arguable position.
  • Evidence that drives the argument instead of the student's own reasoning.
  • Multiple vaguely related claims without a single controlling argument.
  • A conclusion only partially related to your research question.
Sentence frame: Claim: ___. Evidence: ___. This demonstrates ___, which supports my argument that ___.
Row 5

Evidence

0 / 6 / 9 points

Goal: Include relevant, credible, and sufficient evidence — with purposeful analysis — to support your argument.

Do this ✅

  • Select evidence directly relevant to the topic and appropriate for an academic argument.
  • Establish source credibility consistently through attribution, citation, or explanation.
  • Provide purposeful analysis and evaluation of evidence — not just reference or quote it.
  • Use well-vetted sources: scholarly/peer-reviewed, credentialed authors, government or reputable organizations.

Avoid ❌

  • No well-vetted sources beyond the stimulus materials — this risks a 0 for Row 5.
  • Encyclopedias or dictionaries — these do not fulfill the well-vetted source requirement.
  • Outdated research used without explaining why it is still relevant.
  • Treating all sources as equally reliable — evaluate appropriateness for the context.
Sentence frame: This source is credible because ___, and it supports my claim that ___ by showing ___.
Row 6

Citations & Attribution

0 / 3 / 5 points

Goal: Accurately attribute and cite all sources using consistent in-text citations or footnotes linked to a complete bibliography.

📌 College Board does not require a specific style sheet — any consistent, complete style is acceptable. Essential elements: author/organization, title, publication, and date.

Do this ✅

  • Choose any citation style and use it consistently throughout.
  • Include in-text citations or footnotes for all paraphrased and quoted material.
  • Build a bibliography with a clear organizational principle (alphabetical or numerical) and all essential elements.
  • Ensure every in-text reference links clearly to a bibliography entry.

Avoid ❌

  • Having a bibliography but no in-text citations — or vice versa. Both trigger a 0.
  • Vague attributive phrasing alone like "Studies show…" or "Research says…" without a citation.
  • Entries with missing essential elements or unclear URLs that can't be matched to in-text references.
  • No organizational principle in the bibliography (e.g., random order).
Check: Does your in-text reference match a full bibliography entry with author, title, publication, and date?
Row 7

Academic Style

0 / 2 / 3 points

Goal: Create variety, emphasis, and interest through effective sentences and precise word choice, consistently appropriate for an academic audience.

📌 Scorers evaluate only student-generated sentences — not quoted or directly derived material.

Do this ✅

  • Use sentence variety to create emphasis and interest.
  • Choose precise vocabulary sufficient to communicate complex ideas.
  • Maintain a consistently academic tone — a few minor errors are acceptable at 3 pts.
  • Keep prose clear — overly dense sentences that sacrifice coherence lower your score.

Avoid ❌

  • Colloquial or conversational language (can drop you to a 0).
  • Run-on sentences, fragments, or awkward syntax when integrating quoted material.
  • Imprecise word choices that fail to communicate complex ideas.
  • Overly dense prose at the expense of coherence and clarity.
Tip: Replace vague phrases like "a lot" or "I think" with specific, precise academic phrasing.

Row 1 — Stimulus Integration 0 or 5 pts • Understand & Analyze Context

5 points: The response demonstrates the relevance of at least one stimulus by integrating it as part of the argument (as context for the research question or as evidence for a claim). The stimulus is accurately represented, reflects an understanding of its context (date, region, topic), and performs a relevant, authentic function that enhances or directly supports the argument.

0 points: The stimulus is not incorporated, is mentioned in only one sentence, or is discussed but does not contribute to the argument — for example, it is tangential, misrepresents the source, is used only for a definition, or could be deleted with little or no effect on the response.

Row 2 — Context & Significance 0 or 5 pts • Understand & Analyze Context

5 points: The response explains the significance or importance of the research question by situating it within a larger context. The area of investigation is narrow enough to address complexity. Specific and relevant who/what/where/when details are provided for all elements of the question, and a specific, compelling case is made for urgency or importance. The context remains relevant throughout the argument.

0 points: The response provides no context, makes only simplistic or general statements, identifies too many aspects of the topic to address complexity, provides context for only part of the question, or asserts significance without substantiation (e.g., "this is important").

Row 3 — Perspectives 0 / 6 / 9 pts • Understand & Analyze Perspective

📌 A perspective is "a point of view conveyed through an argument" — meaning it must come from a specific source, not a general stakeholder label, topic, or lens.

9 points: The response evaluates multiple perspectives by drawing relevant connections between them, considering objections, implications, and limitations. Source attribution is consistent. Specific details from different sources' arguments are used to explain relationships and evaluate comparative strengths and weaknesses.

6 points: The response describes multiple perspectives and identifies some relevant similarities or differences, but the analysis is general — it explains that agreement or disagreement exists without a nuanced, detailed discussion. Attribution may lapse at times.

0 points: Only a single perspective is presented, perspectives are expressed as opinions without source attribution, perspectives are listed without comparison, or different voices are oversimplified as a single perspective.

Row 4 — Argument 0 / 8 / 12 pts • Establish Argument

📌 The conclusion may also take the form of a resolution or solution, depending on the nature of the question.

12 points: The response is a clear, convincing, logically organized argument driven by student voice (commentary). Claims and evidence are connected through commentary that engages with the content of evidence to draw conclusions. The conclusion is fully aligned with the research question and detailed enough to assess plausibility.

8 points: A claim exists, but the response either organizes the argument well OR links evidence and claims well — but not both. Evidence may drive the argument rather than support it. Claims may lack cohesion, or the conclusion may not be fully aligned with the research question.

0 points: The response provides only unsubstantiated opinions, summarizes information without arguing a position, employs minimal connections between claims and evidence, or presents an argument that is very difficult to discern or contradicts itself.

Row 5 — Evidence 0 / 6 / 9 pts • Select & Use Evidence

📌 General reference guides such as encyclopedias and dictionaries do not fulfill the requirement for a well-vetted source.

9 points: Evidence is relevant, credible, and sufficient to support the argument. Sources are relevant to the topic and appropriate for an academic argument. Credibility is consistently established through attribution, citation, or explanation. Purposeful analysis and evaluation of evidence is provided, with effective use of well-chosen scholarly work.

6 points: Evidence is mostly relevant and credible, but evaluation is uneven or inconsistent. The response may be overly reliant on journalistic sources, may include many sources merely referenced without justification, or may use outdated research without rationale.

0 points: Evidence is predominantly irrelevant or lacks credibility. No well-vetted sources beyond the stimulus materials are included, or a well-vetted source is used but not effectively (trivially selected, misaligned with the claim, or misrepresented).

Row 6 — Citations & Attribution 0 / 3 / 5 pts • Apply Conventions

📌 No specific style sheet is required — any consistent, complete style is acceptable. Essential elements are: author/organization, title, publication, and date.

5 points: The response accurately attributes and cites sources through in-text citations or footnotes. The bibliography uses a consistent style with a clear organizational principle (alphabetical or numerical) and links clearly to in-text references. Attributive phrasing is consistently used for paraphrased material.

3 points: Citations are present but inconsistent or include errors (missing elements, unclear references). The bibliography has some organizational principle that may be uneven in places. Some in-text citations link successfully to bibliography entries, but others are unclear.

0 points: The bibliography is missing, or in-text citations/footnotes are largely missing. Having only one without the other earns a 0. Vague attributive phrasing ("Studies show...") alone with no in-text citation also risks a 0.

Row 7 — Academic Style 0 / 2 / 3 pts • Apply Conventions

📌 Scorers evaluate only student-generated sentences — not quoted or directly derived material.

3 points: The response creates variety, emphasis, and interest through effective sentences and precise word choice. The written style is consistently appropriate for an academic audience. A few minor grammar or style errors are acceptable as long as they do not impede clarity.

2 points: The response is mostly clear but contains some lapses in sentence control (run-ons, fragments, awkward syntax), occasional colloquial language, imprecise word choices, or overly dense prose at the expense of coherence.

0 points: The response has many grammatical flaws that make reading difficult, uses a style that is colloquial or inappropriate for an academic audience, provides too few sentences to evaluate, or student-generated words are indistinguishable from paraphrased source material.

Key Scoring Notes — Read Before You Submit

The IWA is scored row-by-row using the preponderance of evidence for each row independently. A response can receive a 0 in a single row if it falls below the minimum for that row. A response earns a 0 across all rows if it is off-topic, a repetition of the prompt, entirely crossed out, a drawing or other markings, a presentation or other off-task format, or not written in English. A blank submission receives NR (No Response) — not a 0. Citing only prior-year stimulus materials is off-topic. College Board does not require a specific citation style — any consistent, complete style is accepted.
